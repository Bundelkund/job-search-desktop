# Questionnaire Answers — Sam Thornbury

> **Fictional example.** All names, employers, numbers, and details are invented.
> Persona: Sam Thornbury, a product manager transitioning toward AI product strategy roles.

---

## Part 1: Story & Arc

**P1.1** — What was the moment that first pulled you toward your field?

> "Honestly it was watching my mum — a nurse — spend 40 minutes after every shift
> filling in paper forms that tracked nothing useful. I was about 19. I thought: someone
> could fix this. I studied computer science but I was always more interested in whether
> people would use the thing than whether it compiled. That tension between the technical
> and the human became my whole career."

**P1.3** — What problem do you feel called to work on?

> "The gap between what AI systems can do and what the people using them actually
> understand. Most 'AI adoption' projects fail because the product team optimizes for
> capability and ignores mental models. I want to be the person who bridges that."

**P1.8** — How would a close colleague describe what you are really good at?

> "Probably: 'Sam asks the question nobody else thought to ask.' I tend to notice
> the assumption baked into the brief. And then I can usually figure out whether that
> assumption is load-bearing or not."

**P1.5** — Where do you want to be in 3 years?

> "Leading product at a scale-up that is building AI tools for non-technical users.
> I want to own the roadmap, not just contribute to it."

**P1.6** — What are you currently learning?

> "Prompt engineering and AI evaluation design. I run a small internal reading group
> at work on LLM failure modes. I'm also halfway through a course on decision science."

**P1.7** — The 10-year-old dream:

> "Veterinarian. I still don't think there's much of a thread there, honestly — I
> just liked animals. Although I did get a job at 22 building a scheduling tool for
> a veterinary practice, so maybe that's something."

**P1.2** — Career path explained to a 14-year-old:

> "I started building apps that nobody used. Then I got a job where I had to talk to
> the people who were supposed to use them, and I found out why they didn't. Turns out
> I was good at the talking part, so I did more of that. Now I spend most of my time
> figuring out what problem a thing should actually solve before anyone builds it."

**P1.4** — The quiet achievement:

> "Killed a feature three months before launch. Everyone hated me for two weeks.
> Six months later the PM told me it had saved them from shipping a product nobody
> wanted. Never made it onto my CV."

## Part 2: Values & Motivation

**P2.1** — Three non-negotiables:

> "1. I need to see the user — real humans, not analytics. I need to be able to talk
> to the people we're building for at least once a month.
> 2. I need psychological safety to say 'this is wrong' without it becoming political.
> 3. Async-first culture. I do my best thinking alone; endless sync meetings break me."

**P2.2** — Real reason for leaving a previous role:

> "The company said it was 'data-driven' but decisions were actually made by whoever
> was loudest in the room. I kept doing the research and watching it get ignored.
> I didn't say that in the exit interview."

**P2.3** — The manager who brought out the best:

> "Priya. She would ask 'what do you think?' before sharing her own view, every time.
> She gave me a hard brief and then stayed out of the way. When I got something wrong
> she'd say 'what would you do differently' before telling me her answer. I shipped the
> best work of my career in that team."

**P2.5** — Real transition motivation:

> "The company is doing well but the AI roadmap has become a features race. I want to
> be somewhere that takes the 'should we build this?' question seriously, not just
> the 'can we build this?' question."

**P2.6** — Would turn down even a perfect offer if:

> "The interview process showed contempt for candidates. If the process is broken, the
> culture is probably broken in the same way."

**P2.7** — Most 'yourself' at work:

> "Whiteboard session, small group, thorny problem, no slides. Or writing a strategy
> doc alone at 7am. Either extreme."

**P2.4** — Ethical line:

> "Designing dark patterns. Building something I know is manipulative, even if it
> is technically legal. I've declined a brief on those grounds once."

## Part 3: Working Style & Voice

**P3.1** — Natural team role:

> "The one who challenges. Not aggressively — I ask questions rather than make
> declarations. But I'm the person who will say 'I think we're solving the wrong problem'
> when everyone else has committed to the solution."

**P3.2** — Explaining complex things:

> "I use analogies obsessively. When I was explaining embedding space to a marketing
> team I said 'imagine every document is a place on a map, and similar documents are
> near each other — the model just learned the geography.' It's not technically precise
> but it let them ask the right questions."

**P3.4** — Written communication:

> "Natural. I actually prefer writing to speaking for anything important — you can
> edit a document, you can't un-say something in a meeting. I write long but I'm good
> at cutting. My Slack messages are probably too considered."

**P3.5** — Worst professional day:

> "We shipped a feature that broke accessibility for screen-reader users and I hadn't
> flagged the risk early enough. Found out from a user complaint. I spent the afternoon
> fixing it and the evening writing a post-mortem nobody asked for. What I learned: I'm
> bad at raising process concerns proactively when I'm heads-down in delivery."

**P3.6** — Perfectionism:

> "High. It helps with written communication and strategy docs — I will rewrite a brief
> until it's right. It hurts with shipping: I've held back on demos because they weren't
> polished. I've gotten better by setting a 'good enough to learn' bar explicitly."

**P3.3** — Approaching unknown problems:

> "Write down what I think I know and what I think I don't know. Then find the
> assumption I'm most confident about and try to break it. If it breaks, that's
> usually where the real problem is."

## Part 4: STAR Achievement Evidence

**P4.1** — Rally people around an unexciting goal:

> "Quarterly roadmap planning. No one thinks it's exciting. I made it a 2-hour async
> process instead of a 3-day in-person offsite. Built a shared Notion template,
> collected written input from 14 stakeholders, synthesized it into a draft, then
> held a single 60-minute call to resolve conflicts. First cycle took me a day to set
> up. We saved approximately 40 hours of meeting time per quarter and got higher-quality
> input because introverts could write instead of being talked over."

**P4.3** — Fewer resources:

> "Needed user research for a redesign; no research budget. I recruited 8 participants
> from our customer Slack community in exchange for £20 Amazon vouchers, ran the
> sessions myself in the evenings over two weeks, synthesized the findings in a
> one-pager. The redesign shipped with zero additional UX headcount. Conversion on
> the onboarding flow went up 22%."

**P4.8** — Most proud achievement:

> "Killed a product line that was generating £300k ARR but had a 78% annual churn rate.
> The data said it was a zombie — retaining almost no customers past year one. I built
> the case over three months, took it to the CEO, and it got cut. The team hated the
> decision for about six months. Then the freed-up engineering capacity let us ship
> the feature that became our fastest-growing revenue line. I can't claim full credit
> but I'm proud I had the conviction to push for it."

**P4.5** — Challenged a process:

> "Our sprint review format. Everyone presented to the room; nobody pushed back. I
> proposed switching to a 'show, don't tell' format where demos are recorded and shared
> async before the meeting, and the live time is for questions and decisions only.
> Took two sprints to get right but the quality of feedback improved noticeably."

**P4.2** — Failure and recovery:

> "Scoped a project badly and missed a key dependency on a third-party API that had
> a 6-week onboarding process. Discovered this 4 weeks into a 10-week timeline. I
> told the stakeholders immediately, proposed a rescoped MVP that didn't require the
> API, and we shipped that on time. The full version followed 8 weeks later. The
> lesson: I now do a dependency audit before locking any timeline."

## Part 5: Positioning & Target Roles

**P5.1** — Elevator pitch:

> "I help product teams building AI tools figure out what they should be building —
> and what they should stop building — before they spend six months on the wrong thing."

**P5.3** — Target roles:

> "Head of Product or Senior PM at companies building AI-native tools for knowledge
> workers. Ideally series B or C scale-ups, 50–200 people. I want enough structure
> to have good process but enough ambiguity that the strategy isn't already written."

**P5.4** — What most candidates don't bring:

> "I can read an LLM eval and understand what it means, but I can also write
> the user research interview guide. That combination is rare. Most PMs either
> understand the technical side or the human side; I've spent five years deliberately
> building both."

**P5.6** — Non-conventional background reframe:

> "My background is product, not AI research. But AI products fail in the product
> layer, not the model layer — at least at this stage of the market. The failure
> modes I've spent my career on (wrong problem, wrong user, wrong mental model,
> wrong launch) are exactly the failure modes killing AI products right now."
